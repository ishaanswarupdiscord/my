Our Galaxy

Galaxies are made up of stars, dust, and dark matter, all held together by gravity. It’s not scientifically proven how exactly galaxies were formed. After an explosion of matter, space was formed. It is made of hydrogen, helium and other particles.

Nature has provided an extremely large array of galaxies, ranging from faint, diffused to brilliant spiral-shaped giants. Galaxies usually exist in groups, some of which, in turn, are grouped into larger clusters.

The galaxies were not recognized until the early 20th century. Since then, however, galaxies have become one of the very important points of astronomical study.

Types of Galaxies

Galaxies are classified by shape. There are three general types:

  • Elliptical Galaxies: Elliptical galaxies have different sizes, ranging from tens of millions to over one hundred trillion stars.
  • Spiral Galaxies: The Milky Way is a barred spiral, although the bar itself is difficult to observe from Earth's current position within the galactic disc.
  • Irregular Galaxies: These galaxies have no perfect shape or structure. They have formed from collisions with other galaxies or frenzied internal activity.

Fun Facts about Galaxies

  • The greek word for "milky" is the origin of the word galaxy.
  • Most of the mass of a galaxy is made up of an unknown substance called ‘dark matter’. Andromeda is the closest galaxy to the Milky Way.
  • The sun needs two hundred million years to orbit the center of the galaxy. This is called a galactic year.
